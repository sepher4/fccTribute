Ada Lovelace

The first programmer

A potrait of Ada Lovelace

  • December 10, 1815: Ada Lovelace is born in London, England. She is the daughter of the poet Lord Byron and his wife, Anne Isabella Milbanke.
  • 1828: Lovelace meets the mathematician Charles Babbage, who shows her his plans for a "Difference Engine," an early mechanical calculator.
  • 1833: Lovelace begins corresponding with Babbage about his ideas for a more advanced machine, which he calls the "Analytical Engine." She becomes increasingly fascinated by the machine and begins to work on developing a method for using it to perform calculations.
  • 1842: Lovelace translates an article about the Analytical Engine by the Italian mathematician Luigi Menabrea from French to English. She adds extensive notes to the article, which are later published as "Notes on the Analytical Engine." In these notes, Lovelace describes a method for using the machine to calculate Bernoulli numbers, and also speculates about the future potential of computing machines.
  • 1843: Lovelace writes to Babbage about a vision she has had of the Analytical Engine being used to compose music. She suggests that the machine could be programmed to produce music based on mathematical rules, and argues that this would not only be a valuable artistic pursuit, but could also lead to new insights into the nature of music and mathematics.
  • 1852: Lovelace dies of cancer at the age of 36. Her contributions to the development of computing are largely forgotten for many years, but begin to be rediscovered in the 20th century.
  • 1980: The United States Department of Defense creates a new computer programming language and names it "Ada" in honor of Lovelace.
  • 1991: The Association for Women in Computing establishes the Ada Lovelace Award to recognize women who have made significant contributions to the field of computing.
  • 2009: Lovelace is posthumously inducted into the Women's Hall of Fame in Seneca Falls, New York.
  • 2015: Lovelace's bicentennial birthday is celebrated with a variety of events, including a Google Doodle and a conference at the University of Oxford.
